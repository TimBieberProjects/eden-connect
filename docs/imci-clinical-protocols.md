# WHO IMCI Clinical Protocols for Community Health Workers
## Integrated Management of Childhood Illness — PNG Context
### Source: WHO IMCI Chart Booklet (March 2014), PNG Malaria Treatment Protocol

---

## OVERVIEW OF THE IMCI PROCESS

IMCI uses a **colour-coded triage classification**:
- **PINK** = Urgent hospital referral. Give pre-referral treatment immediately.
- **YELLOW** = Specific outpatient treatment needed. Give first dose in clinic, teach mother.
- **GREEN** = Home care with counselling. Advise mother, give follow-up date.

Assessment always covers: General danger signs → Main symptoms (cough, diarrhoea, fever, ear) → Malnutrition/anaemia → HIV status → Immunisation status.

---

## PART 1: SICK CHILD — AGE 2 MONTHS UP TO 5 YEARS

---

### STEP 1: CHECK FOR GENERAL DANGER SIGNS

Ask and look for ANY of these four signs:

| Danger Sign | How to Assess |
|---|---|
| Not able to drink or breastfeed | Ask mother |
| Vomits everything | Ask mother |
| Convulsions (now or in this illness) | Ask mother; look |
| Lethargic or unconscious | Look — child does not respond normally |

**If ANY danger sign present:**
- Classification: **PINK — VERY SEVERE DISEASE**
- Give diazepam if convulsing now (see dosage below)
- Complete the full assessment quickly
- Give any pre-referral treatment immediately
- Treat to prevent low blood sugar
- Keep child warm
- **REFER URGENTLY**

---

### STEP 2: ASSESS AND CLASSIFY — COUGH OR DIFFICULT BREATHING

**Ask:** Does the child have cough or difficult breathing? If yes, for how long?

**Look, listen, feel (child must be calm):**
- Count breaths in ONE FULL MINUTE
- Look for chest indrawing (lower chest wall goes IN when child breathes IN)
- Look and listen for stridor (harsh noise on breathing IN)
- Look and listen for wheezing

**Fast breathing thresholds:**
| Age | Fast breathing = |
|---|---|
| 2 months up to 12 months | 50 breaths/minute or more |
| 12 months up to 5 years | 40 breaths/minute or more |

*If pulse oximeter available: refer if oxygen saturation < 90%*

#### Classification Table — Cough or Difficult Breathing

| Signs | Classification | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Any GENERAL DANGER SIGN, OR stridor in calm child | **PINK: SEVERE PNEUMONIA OR VERY SEVERE DISEASE** | Give first dose of appropriate antibiotic. Refer URGENTLY to hospital. |
| Chest indrawing, OR fast breathing | **YELLOW: PNEUMONIA** | Oral Amoxicillin 5 days. If wheezing: inhaled bronchodilator 5 days. If chest indrawing in HIV-exposed/infected child: give first dose amoxicillin and refer. Soothe throat. If cough >14 days or recurrent wheeze: refer for TB/asthma. Follow-up in 3 days. |
| No signs of pneumonia or very severe disease | **GREEN: COUGH OR COLD** | If wheezing: inhaled bronchodilator 5 days. Soothe throat. If cough >14 days or recurrent wheeze: refer for TB/asthma. Follow-up in 5 days if not improving. |

**If wheezing present:** Give rapid-acting inhaled bronchodilator (salbutamol) up to 3 times, 15–20 minutes apart. Re-count breaths and re-check chest indrawing before classifying.

---

### STEP 3: ASSESS AND CLASSIFY — DIARRHOEA

**Ask:** Does the child have diarrhoea? If yes, how long? Any blood in stool?

**Look and feel:**
- Child's general condition: lethargic/unconscious? Restless and irritable?
- Look for sunken eyes
- Offer fluid: not able to drink or drinking poorly? Drinking eagerly, thirsty?
- Pinch abdominal skin: goes back very slowly (>2 sec)? Or slowly?

#### Classification Table — Dehydration

| Signs (need TWO) | Classification | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Lethargic/unconscious + sunken eyes + not able to drink/drinking poorly + skin pinch goes back very slowly | **PINK: SEVERE DEHYDRATION** | If no other severe classification: give IV/NG fluid (Plan C). If other severe classification: refer URGENTLY with ORS sips on the way. Continue breastfeeding. If ≥2 years and cholera in area: give antibiotic for cholera. |
| Restless/irritable + sunken eyes + drinks eagerly/thirsty + skin pinch goes back slowly | **YELLOW: SOME DEHYDRATION** | Give ORS, zinc supplements, food (Plan B). If other severe classification: refer URGENTLY with ORS on the way. Follow-up in 5 days. |
| Not enough signs for above | **GREEN: NO DEHYDRATION** | Give fluid, zinc, food at home (Plan A). Follow-up in 5 days if not improving. |

#### Classification Table — Diarrhoea Duration and Type

| Signs | Classification | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Diarrhoea ≥14 days + dehydration present | **PINK: SEVERE PERSISTENT DIARRHOEA** | Treat dehydration before referral (unless other severe classification). Refer to hospital. |
| Diarrhoea ≥14 days + no dehydration | **YELLOW: PERSISTENT DIARRHOEA** | Feed child as for persistent diarrhoea. Multivitamins + minerals (including zinc) for 14 days. Follow-up in 5 days. |
| Blood in stool | **YELLOW: DYSENTERY** | Give ciprofloxacin 3 days. Follow-up in 3 days. |

---

### STEP 4: ASSESS AND CLASSIFY — FEVER

**Ask:** Does the child have fever (by history, feels hot, or temperature ≥37.5°C)?

If yes:
- Decide malaria risk: HIGH or LOW (based on local epidemiology)
- Ask: how long? If >7 days, every day? Measles in last 3 months?
- Look for: stiff neck, runny nose, bacterial cause of fever
- Look for signs of MEASLES: generalised rash + one of (cough, runny nose, red eyes)

**Do malaria test (RDT):**
- HIGH malaria risk: test ALL fever cases
- LOW malaria risk: test if no obvious cause of fever
- If no test available: HIGH risk = classify as MALARIA; LOW risk + no obvious cause = classify as MALARIA

**PNG MALARIA NOTE:** PNG first-line treatment for uncomplicated P. falciparum = **Artemether-Lumefantrine (AL)**. First-line for P. vivax = AL + Primaquine. Severe malaria = Artesunate injection then AL.

#### Classification Table — Fever (with malaria risk)

| Signs | Classification | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Any general danger sign OR stiff neck | **PINK: VERY SEVERE FEBRILE DISEASE** | Give first dose artesunate or quinine for severe malaria. Give first dose appropriate antibiotic. Treat to prevent low blood sugar. Paracetamol if ≥38.5°C. Refer URGENTLY. |
| Malaria test POSITIVE | **YELLOW: MALARIA** | Give recommended first-line oral antimalarial (AL in PNG). Paracetamol if high fever. Treat bacterial cause if identified. Return in 3 days if fever persists; refer if fever every day for >7 days. |
| Malaria test NEGATIVE + other cause of fever present | **GREEN: FEVER — NO MALARIA** | Paracetamol if high fever. Treat bacterial cause. Return in 3 days if fever persists. Refer if fever every day >7 days. |
| No malaria risk and no travel to malaria risk area | **GREEN: FEVER — NO MALARIA** | Paracetamol if high fever. Treat bacterial cause. Return in 2 days if fever persists. |

#### Classification Table — Measles (if measles now or within last 3 months)

| Signs | Classification | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Any general danger sign OR clouding of cornea OR deep/extensive mouth ulcers | **PINK: SEVERE COMPLICATED MEASLES** | Vitamin A treatment. First dose appropriate antibiotic. If corneal clouding or eye pus: tetracycline eye ointment. Refer URGENTLY. |
| Pus draining from eye OR mouth ulcers | **YELLOW: MEASLES WITH EYE OR MOUTH COMPLICATIONS** | Vitamin A treatment. If eye pus: tetracycline eye ointment. If mouth ulcers: gentian violet. Follow-up in 3 days. |
| Measles now or within last 3 months, no above signs | **GREEN: MEASLES** | Vitamin A treatment. |

---

### STEP 5: ASSESS AND CLASSIFY — EAR PROBLEM

**Ask:** Ear pain? Ear discharge? If yes, how long?

**Look and feel:** Pus draining from ear? Tender swelling behind ear (mastoid)?

| Signs | Classification | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Tender swelling behind the ear | **PINK: MASTOIDITIS** | First dose antibiotic. Paracetamol for pain. Refer URGENTLY. |
| Pus draining <14 days, OR ear pain | **YELLOW: ACUTE EAR INFECTION** | Antibiotic 5 days. Paracetamol for pain. Dry ear by wicking. Follow-up in 5 days. |
| Pus draining ≥14 days | **YELLOW: CHRONIC EAR INFECTION** | Dry ear by wicking. Topical quinolone eardrops for 14 days. Follow-up in 5 days. |
| No ear pain, no pus | **GREEN: NO EAR INFECTION** | No treatment. |

---

### STEP 6: CHECK FOR ACUTE MALNUTRITION

**Look and feel:**
- Oedema of both feet
- Weight-for-Height/Length (WFH/L) z-score
- MUAC (Mid-Upper Arm Circumference) for children ≥6 months

**If WFH/L < -3 z-scores OR MUAC < 115 mm, check for:**
- Medical complications (any general danger sign, any severe classification, pneumonia with chest indrawing)
- If ≥6 months: offer RUTF (Ready-to-Use Therapeutic Food) — can child finish at least 1/3 packet (92g) in 30 min?
- If <6 months: assess breastfeeding problem

| Signs | Classification | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Oedema of both feet, OR WFH/L < -3 z OR MUAC < 115mm AND (medical complication, OR not able to finish RUTF, OR breastfeeding problem) | **PINK: COMPLICATED SEVERE ACUTE MALNUTRITION** | First dose antibiotic. Treat to prevent low blood sugar. Keep warm. Refer URGENTLY. |
| WFH/L < -3 z OR MUAC < 115mm, NO complications, passes appetite test | **YELLOW: UNCOMPLICATED SEVERE ACUTE MALNUTRITION** | Oral antibiotics 5 days. RUTF for child ≥6 months. Counsel mother on feeding. Assess for TB. Follow-up in 7 days. |
| WFH/L between -3 and -2 z, OR MUAC 115–124 mm | **YELLOW: MODERATE ACUTE MALNUTRITION** | Assess feeding and counsel mother. Follow-up feeding problem in 7 days; routine follow-up in 30 days. Assess for TB. |
| WFH/L ≥ -2 z OR MUAC ≥ 125 mm | **GREEN: NO ACUTE MALNUTRITION** | If <2 years: assess feeding and counsel. |

**MUAC thresholds:**
- < 115 mm = Severe Acute Malnutrition
- 115–124 mm = Moderate Acute Malnutrition
- ≥ 125 mm = No Acute Malnutrition

---

### STEP 7: CHECK FOR ANAEMIA

**Look:** Palmar pallor (look at child's open palm)

| Signs | Classification | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Severe palmar pallor | **PINK: SEVERE ANAEMIA** | Refer URGENTLY to hospital. |
| Some palmar pallor | **YELLOW: ANAEMIA** | Give iron 14 days. Mebendazole if ≥1 year and no dose in last 6 months. Follow-up in 14 days. |
| No palmar pallor | **GREEN: NO ANAEMIA** | If <2 years: assess feeding and counsel. |

*Note: If severe acute malnutrition on RUTF, do NOT give iron (RUTF already contains adequate iron).*

## PART 2: TREATMENT PROTOCOLS

### Oral Drug Dosages — Antibiotics

#### Amoxicillin (First-line for pneumonia and acute ear infection)
**Give TWO times daily for 5 days**

| Age / Weight | Tablet 250mg | Syrup 250mg/5ml |
|---|---|---|
| 2 months – <12 months (4–<10 kg) | 1 tablet | 5 ml |
| 12 months – <3 years (10–<14 kg) | 2 tablets | 10 ml |
| 3 years – 5 years (14–19 kg) | 3 tablets | 15 ml |

#### Ciprofloxacin (For dysentery)
**Give 15mg/kg TWO times daily for 3 days**

| Age | 250mg tablet | 500mg tablet |
|---|---|---|
| <6 months | 1/2 | 1/4 |
| 6 months – 5 years | 1 | 1/2 |

#### Cotrimoxazole (HIV prophylaxis — once daily)

| Age | Syrup 40/200mg/5ml | Paediatric tablet 20/100mg | Adult tablet 80/400mg |
|---|---|---|---|
| <6 months | 2.5 ml | 1 | — |
| 6 months – 5 years | 5 ml | 2 | 1/2 |

### Antimalarial Dosages

#### Artemether-Lumefantrine (AL) — First-line for malaria in PNG
**Twice daily for 3 days. Give first dose in clinic, observe 1 hour. If vomits within 1 hour: repeat dose. Give with food.**

| Weight (Age) | Tablets (20mg artemether/120mg lumefantrine) per dose |
|---|---|
| 5–<10 kg (2 months – 12 months) | 1 tablet |
| 10–<14 kg (12 months – 3 years) | 1 tablet |
| 14–<19 kg (3 years – 5 years) | 2 tablets |

#### Artesunate + Amodiaquine (AS+AQ) — Once daily for 3 days (alternative)

| Weight | AS/AQ (25mg/67.5mg) | AS/AQ (50mg/135mg) |
|---|---|---|
| 5–<10 kg | 1 tablet/day | — |
| 10–<14 kg | — | 1 tablet/day |
| 14–<19 kg | — | 1 tablet/day |

### Paracetamol (For high fever ≥38.5°C or ear pain)
**Give every 6 hours until high fever or ear pain gone**

| Age / Weight | Tablet 100mg | Tablet 500mg |
|---|---|---|
| 2 months – 3 years (4–<14 kg) | 1 tablet | 1/4 tablet |
| 3 years – 5 years (14–<19 kg) | 1.5 tablets | 1/2 tablet |

### Iron (For anaemia — give daily for 14 days)

| Age / Weight | Iron/Folate tablet (ferrous sulfate 200mg + folate) | Iron syrup (ferrous fumarate 100mg/5ml = 20mg elemental iron/ml) |
|---|---|---|
| 2–4 months (4–<6 kg) | — | 1.00 ml |
| 4–12 months (6–<10 kg) | — | 1.25 ml |
| 12 months – 3 years (10–<14 kg) | 1/2 tablet | 2.00 ml |
| 3–5 years (14–19 kg) | 1/2 tablet | 2.5 ml |

### Vitamin A

| Age | Dose |
|---|---|
| 6–12 months | 100,000 IU |
| ≥1 year | 200,000 IU |

Give every 6 months to all children from 6 months. Extra dose for measles or persistent diarrhoea (unless dose in past month or on RUTF).

### Mebendazole
- 500 mg single dose in clinic
- Give if: child ≥1 year, hookworm/whipworm problem in area, no dose in last 6 months

## PART 3: CLINIC-ONLY TREATMENTS

### Intramuscular Antibiotics (For urgent referral)
Give both Ampicillin AND Gentamicin

**Ampicillin (50mg/kg IM)** — Dilute 500mg vial with 2.1ml sterile water (= 500mg/2.5ml)

| Age | Dose |
|---|---|
| 2–4 months (4–<6 kg) | 1 ml |
| 4–12 months (6–<10 kg) | 2 ml |
| 12 months – 3 years (10–<14 kg) | 3 ml |
| 3–5 years (14–19 kg) | 5 ml |

**Gentamicin (7.5mg/kg IM, once daily)**

| Age | Dose |
|---|---|
| 2–4 months (4–<6 kg) | 0.5–1.0 ml |
| 4–12 months (6–<10 kg) | 1.1–1.8 ml |
| 12 months – 3 years (10–<14 kg) | 1.9–2.7 ml |
| 3–5 years (14–19 kg) | 2.8–3.5 ml |

If referral delayed: repeat Ampicillin every 6 hours. If meningitis suspected: increase Ampicillin dose x4.

### Diazepam (For convulsions — rectal administration)
Give 0.5mg/kg rectal using syringe without needle. Repeat after 10 minutes if convulsions continue. Give oxygen.

| Age / Weight | Dose (10mg/2ml solution) |
|---|---|
| 2–6 months (5–7 kg) | 0.5 ml |
| 6–12 months (7–<10 kg) | 1.0 ml |
| 12 months – 3 years (10–<14 kg) | 1.5 ml |
| 3–5 years (14–19 kg) | 2.0 ml |

### Artesunate / Quinine for Severe Malaria (Clinic/pre-referral only)

**Rectal Artesunate Suppository (10mg/kg)**

| Age / Weight | 50mg suppository | 200mg suppository |
|---|---|---|
| 2–4 months (4–<6 kg) | 1 | — |
| 4–12 months (6–<10 kg) | 2 | — |
| 12 months – 2 years (10–<12 kg) | 2 | — |
| 2–3 years (12–<14 kg) | 3 | 1 |
| 3–5 years (14–19 kg) | 3 | 1 |

**IM Artesunate (2.4mg/kg, 60mg vial at 20mg/ml)**

| Age / Weight | Dose |
|---|---|
| 2–4 months (4–<6 kg) | 0.4 ml |
| 4–12 months (6–<10 kg) | 0.6 ml |
| 12 months – 2 years (10–<12 kg) | 0.8 ml |
| 2–3 years (12–<14 kg) | 1.0 ml |
| 3–5 years (14–19 kg) | 1.2 ml |

**IM Quinine** — if artesunate not available. Give first dose; repeat at 4 and 8 hours, then every 12 hours until child can take oral. Do not give to child <4 months in low malaria risk area.

### Prevent Low Blood Sugar (Before/during referral)
- If can breastfeed: put to breast
- If can swallow but not breastfeed: 30–50 ml expressed breast milk or sugar water
- If cannot swallow: 50 ml by NG tube, or 1 teaspoon sugar (with 1–2 drops water) sublingual, repeat every 20 minutes
- **Sugar water recipe: dissolve 4 level teaspoons sugar (20g) in 200 ml clean water**

## PART 4: ORAL REHYDRATION THERAPY (ORS)

### Plan A — Treat Diarrhoea at Home (No Dehydration)
**4 Rules:**
1. Give extra fluid — breastfeed more; if not exclusive breastfeed add ORS/clean water/soup/yoghurt drink
   - <2 years: 50–100 ml ORS after each loose stool
   - ≥2 years: 100–200 ml ORS after each loose stool
2. Give Zinc supplements (age 2 months – 5 years):
   - 2–6 months: 1/2 tablet (10mg) daily for 14 days
   - ≥6 months: 1 tablet (20mg) daily for 14 days
3. Continue feeding (exclusive breastfeed if <6 months)
4. When to return: blood in stool, drinking poorly, becomes sicker

### Plan B — Treat Some Dehydration with ORS (4 hours in clinic)

| Weight / Age | Total ORS in first 4 hours |
|---|---|
| <6 kg (up to 4 months) | 200–450 ml |
| 6–<10 kg (4 months – 12 months) | 450–800 ml |
| 10–<12 kg (12 months – 2 years) | 800–960 ml |
| 12–19 kg (2–5 years) | 960–1,600 ml |

*Formula: child's weight (kg) × 75 ml = approximate ORS needed*

- Give frequent small sips; if vomits wait 10 min then continue slowly
- After 4 hours: reassess and reclassify
- Provide 2 extra ORS packets for home use

### Plan C — Treat Severe Dehydration Quickly (IV/NG)
- IV Ringer's Lactate (or normal saline) 100 ml/kg:
  - Infants (<12 months): 30 ml/kg in 1 hour, then 70 ml/kg in 5 hours
  - Children (12 months–5 years): 30 ml/kg in 30 minutes, then 70 ml/kg in 2.5 hours
- If no IV: NG tube — 20 ml/kg/hour for 6 hours (total 120 ml/kg)
- Also give ORS 5 ml/kg/hour as soon as child can drink
- Reassess every 1–2 hours

## PART 5: LOCAL INFECTION TREATMENT (Teach Mother at Home)

### Soothe Throat / Relieve Cough
- Breastmilk for breastfed infant
- Safe home remedies (locally determined) — NO harmful remedies

### Mouth Ulcers — Gentian Violet (Half-strength 0.25%)
- Twice daily for 5 days (continue 48 hrs after ulcers heal)
- Wash mouth with clean cloth + salt water first
- Paint with half-strength GV; wash hands

### Thrush — Nystatin
- 4 times daily for 7 days; 1 ml nystatin instilled; no feeding for 20 min after

### Eye Infection — Tetracycline Eye Ointment
- Clean both eyes with clean cloth + water to remove pus
- Apply tetracycline ointment inside lower lid, both eyes, 4 times daily
- Continue until no pus

### Ear — Dry Wicking + Eardrops (Quinolone)
- Roll clean cloth or tissue into wick; place in ear; remove when wet; repeat until dry
- ≥3 times daily
- Instil quinolone eardrops (ciprofloxacin/norfloxacin/ofloxacin) after wicking, 3 times daily for 14 days

### Skin Pustules or Umbilical Infection — Gentian Violet (Full-strength 0.5%)
- Twice daily for 5 days
- Wash with soap and water, dry, paint with full-strength GV (0.5%)

## PART 6: INHALED SALBUTAMOL FOR WHEEZING

**Salbutamol metered dose inhaler — ALWAYS use with spacer for children <5 years**

- Give 2 puffs (200 mcg) via spacer
- Can repeat up to 3 times, 15–20 minutes apart
- Reassess fast breathing and chest indrawing before classifying

**Homemade spacer:** Cut a 500ml plastic bottle, insert inhaler mouthpiece at base. Cut V-shape at open end to fit child's nose. Soften edges with flame.

## PART 8: FOLLOW-UP SCHEDULE

| Condition | Return for follow-up |
|---|---|
| Pneumonia, Dysentery, Malaria (if fever persists), Measles with complications, Mouth ulcers/thrush | 3 days |
| Persistent diarrhoea, Acute ear infection, Chronic ear infection, Cough/cold (not improving) | 5 days |
| Uncomplicated severe acute malnutrition, Feeding problem | 14 days |
| Anaemia | 14 days (continue iron every 14 days for 2 months) |
| Moderate acute malnutrition | 30 days |
| Confirmed HIV infection, HIV exposed | Per national guidelines |

### Return IMMEDIATELY if:
- Not able to drink or breastfeed
- Becomes sicker
- Develops a fever
- **Cough/cold:** fast breathing or difficult breathing
- **Diarrhoea:** blood in stool or drinking poorly

## PART 9: SICK YOUNG INFANT — AGE UP TO 2 MONTHS

### Check for Very Severe Disease and Local Bacterial Infection

**Ask:** Difficulty feeding? Convulsions?

**Look, listen, feel (infant must be calm):**
- Count breaths — if >60/min, repeat count. Fast breathing = ≥60 breaths/min
- Severe chest indrawing
- Temperature: fever ≥37.5°C axillary OR low body temperature <35.5°C axillary
- Umbilicus: red or draining pus?
- Skin pustules?
- Movement: moves on own? Only when stimulated? Does not move at all?

| Signs | Classification | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Any ONE of: not feeding well, convulsions, fast breathing (≥60/min), severe chest indrawing, fever, low temperature, movement only when stimulated or no movement | **PINK: VERY SEVERE DISEASE** | Give IM antibiotics (ampicillin + gentamicin). Treat to prevent low blood sugar. Keep warm. Refer URGENTLY. |
| Umbilicus red or draining pus; skin pustules | **YELLOW: LOCAL BACTERIAL INFECTION** | Oral antibiotic 5 days. Teach mother to treat local infection. Follow-up in 2 days. |
| None of the above signs | **GREEN: SEVERE DISEASE/LOCAL INFECTION UNLIKELY** | Advise home care. |

### Jaundice in Young Infant

| Signs | Classification | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Jaundice appearing <24 hours of age OR yellow palms and soles at any age | **PINK: SEVERE JAUNDICE** | Treat to prevent low blood sugar. Keep warm. Refer URGENTLY. |
| Jaundice appearing after 24 hours, palms/soles not yellow | **YELLOW: JAUNDICE** | Home care. Return immediately if palms/soles become yellow. If >14 days old: refer for assessment. Follow-up in 1 day. |
| No jaundice | **GREEN: NO JAUNDICE** | Home care. |

| Weight | Dose |
|---|---|
| 1–<1.5 kg | 0.4 ml |
| 1.5–<2 kg | 0.5 ml |
| 2–<2.5 kg | 0.7 ml |
| 2.5–<3 kg | 0.8 ml |
| 3–<3.5 kg | 1.0 ml |
| 3.5–<4 kg | 1.1 ml |
| 4–<4.5 kg | 1.3 ml |

**Gentamicin IM** (dilute 80mg vial with 6ml sterile water = 10mg/ml)

| Weight | <7 days (5mg/kg) | ≥7 days (7.5mg/kg) |
|---|---|---|
| 1–<1.5 kg | 0.6 ml | 0.9 ml |
| 1.5–<2 kg | 0.9 ml | 1.3 ml |
| 2–<2.5 kg | 1.1 ml | 1.7 ml |
| 2.5–<3 kg | 1.4 ml | 2.0 ml |
| 3–<3.5 kg | 1.6 ml | 2.4 ml |
| 3.5–<4 kg | 1.9 ml | 2.8 ml |
| 4–<4.5 kg | 2.1 ml | 3.2 ml |

If referral not possible: continue ampicillin (twice daily if <1 week old; three times daily if ≥1 week) + gentamicin once daily for at least 5 days.

### Young Infant — Oral Amoxicillin for Local Bacterial Infection
**Twice daily for 5 days**

| Age / Weight | Tablet 250mg | Syrup 125mg/5ml |
|---|---|---|
| Birth – 1 month (<4 kg) | 1/4 | 2.5 ml |
| 1–2 months (4–<6 kg) | 1/2 | 5 ml |

### Young Infant — When to Return IMMEDIATELY
- Breastfeeding poorly
- Reduced activity
- Becomes sicker
- Develops a fever
- Feels unusually cold
- Fast breathing or difficult breathing
- Palms and soles appear yellow

### Young Infant — PMTCT ARV Prophylaxis (HIV-exposed)

| Birth weight | Nevirapine (once daily) | Zidovudine (once daily) |
|---|---|---|
| 2000–2499 g | 10 mg | 10 mg |
| ≥2500 g | 15 mg | 15 mg |

- If breastfeeding: give NVP for 6 weeks from birth
- If not breastfeeding: give NVP or AZT for 4–6 weeks from birth

## PART 10: SKIN PROBLEMS — IDENTIFICATION AND TREATMENT

| Skin Problem | Signs | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Prurigo (papular itching rash) | Itchy papules, scratch marks, dark spots with pale centres | Calamine lotion; antihistamine oral; if not improving: 1% hydrocortisone. Note: can be early HIV sign. |
| Ringworm (Tinea) | Circular itchy lesion, raised edge, scaly centre, hair loss | Whitfield's ointment or antifungal cream (few patches). If extensive: ketoconazole (6–12 months: 40mg/day; 12 months–5 years: 60mg/day) or griseofulvin 10mg/kg/day. Shave hair if scalp involved. |
| Scabies | Rash + excoriations on torso; burrows in web space/wrists; face spared | Antihistamine; 25% benzyl benzoate topically at night × 3 days; OR 1% lindane cream/lotion (wash off after 12 hours). Note: HIV+ patients may have crusted scabies. |
| Chickenpox | Vesicles over body, appear progressively, form scabs after rupture | Treat itching (antihistamine, calamine). Refer URGENTLY if pneumonia or jaundice appear. |
| Herpes Zoster | Vesicles one side of body, intense pain, uncommon in children unless immunocompromised (HIV) | Keep lesions clean and dry; local antiseptic. If eye involved: acyclovir 20mg/kg × 4 times daily × 5 days. Pain relief. Follow-up in 7 days. |
| Impetigo/Folliculitis | Red, tender, warm crusts or small lesions | Clean sores with antiseptic; drain pus if fluctuant. Cloxacillin if size >4cm or red streaks or tender lymph nodes or multiple abscesses: 25–50mg/kg every 6 hours × 5 days. Refer URGENTLY if fever or infection extends to muscle. |
| Molluscum Contagiosum | Pearly white papules with central umbilication, face/trunk | Can leave alone; phenol dab; electrodesiccation; liquid nitrogen. Note: >100 lesions, giant molluscum, or chronic suggests HIV (clinical stage 2). |
| Warts | Papules/nodules with rough surface | Topical salicylic acid; liquid nitrogen; electrocautery. Extensive warts = HIV stage 2. |
| Seborrhoea | Greasy scales and redness on central face/body folds | Ketoconazole shampoo; 1% hydrocortisone cream × 2 daily. Refer if severe. Note: may be severe in HIV. |
| Fixed drug reaction | Generalised red rash, small bumps/blisters, or dark skin patches | Stop medication; oral antihistamines; if peeling rash: refer. |
| Stevens-Johnson Syndrome | Severe reaction (cotrimoxazole or NVP) — skin, eyes, AND mouth involved, may cause difficulty breathing | Stop medication; REFER URGENTLY. Most lethal reaction to NVP, cotrimoxazole, or efavirenz. |

## PART 11: FEEDING RECOMMENDATIONS (Age-Appropriate)

| Age | Key Recommendations |
|---|---|
| Birth – 1 week | Skin-to-skin immediately after birth. Breastfeed within first hour. Give colostrum. ≥8 feeds/24 hours. No other foods or fluids (especially critical for HIV-positive mothers). |
| 1 week – 6 months | Exclusive breastfeeding. ≥8 feeds/24 hours including night feeds. No other foods. |
| 6–9 months | Breastfeed + thick porridge/mashed foods including animal source + vitamin A-rich fruits/vegetables. 2–3 tablespoons gradually up to 1/2 cup (250ml). 2–3 meals + 1–2 snacks/day. |
| 9–12 months | Breastfeed + mashed/finely chopped family food including animal source + vitamin A-rich foods. 1/2 cup (250ml) per meal. 3–4 meals + 1–2 snacks/day. |
| 12 months – 2 years | Continue breastfeeding + variety of family foods. 3/4 cup per meal. 3–4 meals + 1–2 snacks/day. Feed slowly and patiently. |
| 2 years + | Variety of family foods. At least 1 full cup (250ml) per meal. 3–4 meals + 1–2 snacks/day. |

**A good daily diet includes:** energy-rich food (thick cereal with added oil) + meat/fish/eggs/pulses + fruits and vegetables.

### Feeding During Persistent Diarrhoea
- If breastfeeding: more frequent/longer breastfeeds day and night
- If taking other milk: replace with increased breastfeeding OR fermented milk (yoghurt) OR replace half milk with nutrient-rich semisolid food

## PART 12: PNG-SPECIFIC CONTEXT FOR HEALTH WORKERS

### Disease Burden in PNG
- Leading causes of child morbidity: pneumonia, malaria, tuberculosis, diarrhoeal diseases, meningitis, HIV/AIDS
- PNG has 10th highest TB incidence in the world
- Triple burden: TB + MDR-TB + TB-HIV co-infection
- ~85% of population lives in rural/remote areas

### Malaria in PNG
- **P. falciparum** and **P. vivax** both prevalent
- First-line treatment (since 2011 NMTP): **Artemether-Lumefantrine (AL)**
- Uncomplicated P. vivax: AL + Primaquine
- Severe malaria: Artesunate injection then oral AL
- Second-line (dihydroartemisinin-piperaquine) may be used where available
- **All fever/suspected malaria MUST be tested by RDT or microscopy before treatment**
- Aid posts (lower level): RDT and AL may have limited availability — ensure stock

### Tuberculosis in PNG
- Community-based Directly Observed Treatment (DOTS) is the standard
- Fixed-dose combination therapy reduces pill burden and MDR risk
- Treatment supporters (family/community members) oversee treatment in rural settings
- MDR-TB documented in Eastern Highlands, Gulf, Madang, Milne Bay, Morobe, Western Provinces
- Any child with persistent cough + fever + failure to thrive: **refer for TB assessment**
- IMCI flag: Uncomplicated SAM or moderate malnutrition cases → assess for possible TB infection

### HIV in PNG
- PMTCT: All HIV-positive pregnant/breastfeeding women on lifelong triple ART
- Infants of HIV-positive mothers: NVP prophylaxis from birth
- Cotrimoxazole prophylaxis from 4–6 weeks for HIV-exposed/infected infants
- Virological test (PCR) at 4–6 weeks for HIV-exposed infants
- Mixed feeding increases MTCT risk — exclusive breastfeeding recommended if on ARVs

### CHW Scope in PNG
- 2-year certificate program with 25 competency benchmarks
- Manage: minor illnesses, first aid, simple infections, immunisations, maternal/child health
- Refer: severe illness, any general danger sign, severe classifications in IMCI
- Dispensing authority at aid post level: limited essential medicines (cotrimoxazole, amoxicillin, ORS, paracetamol, antimalarials based on RDT)
- Text message reminder systems have been used to reinforce malaria protocol adherence

### Immunisation Schedule for PNG

| Age | Vaccines |
|---|---|
| Birth | BCG, OPV-0, Hep B0 |
| 6 weeks | DPT+HIB-1, OPV-1, Hep B1, RTV1, PCV1 |
| 10 weeks | DPT+HIB-2, OPV-2, Hep B2, RTV2, PCV2 |
| 14 weeks | DPT+HIB-3, OPV-3, Hep B3, RTV3, PCV3 |
| 9 months | Measles-1 |
| 18 months | DPT booster, Measles-2 |

**Vitamin A:** Every 6 months from age 6 months (100,000 IU for 6–12 months; 200,000 IU for ≥1 year)
**Mebendazole:** 500 mg every 6 months from age 1 year

## PART 13: QUICK REFERENCE — WHEN TO REFER URGENTLY

Refer URGENTLY any child with:
- Any GENERAL DANGER SIGN (unable to drink/breastfeed, vomits everything, convulsions, lethargic/unconscious)
- PINK classification in any category
- Very severe febrile disease
- Severe pneumonia (stridor, general danger sign)
- Severe dehydration (Plan C needed, other severe classification)
- Severe anaemia
- Complicated severe acute malnutrition
- Mastoiditis
- Severe complicated measles
- Young infant with very severe disease
- Young infant with severe jaundice
- HIV child with ART severe side effects (yellow eyes, severe rash, difficulty breathing + severe abdominal pain, fever + vomiting + rash on abacavir)

**Before referral ALWAYS:**
1. Give first dose of appropriate antibiotic (IM if severe)
2. Give artesunate or quinine if severe malaria suspected
3. Give diazepam if convulsing
4. Treat/prevent low blood sugar
5. Keep child warm
6. Give ORS sips on the way if diarrhoea/dehydration
7. Advise mother what to expect at hospital
